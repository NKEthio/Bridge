Lesson 1.1: Advanced Modals and Subjunctive

Modal Verbs for Deduction:

Present Deduction:
  • must + base verb (strong certainty): He must be tired.
  • might/may/could + base verb (possibility): She might arrive late.
  • can't + base verb (impossibility): That can't be true.

Past Deduction:
  • must have + past participle: He must have forgotten.
  • might/may/could have + past participle: She might have missed the bus.

Subjunctive Mood:

Used for wishes, suggestions, and hypothetical situations:
  • I suggest that he study harder. (not "studies")
  • It's essential that she be on time. (not "is")
  • If I were you, I would apply. (not "was")

Lesson 1.2: Inversion and Emphasis

Inversion: Reversing subject and verb for emphasis or formality.

After Negative Adverbs:
  • Never have I seen such beauty.
  • Rarely does he complain.
  • Not only did she pass, but she also got the highest score.
  • Hardly had I arrived when the phone rang.

After "Only":
  • Only then did I understand.
  • Only by working hard can you succeed.
Practice: Rewrite using inversion:
1. I have seldom heard such a beautiful voice.
2. She not only won the competition but also broke the record.

Lesson 1.3: Complex Sentence Structures

Relative Clauses:

Defining (essential information):
The book that you recommended was excellent.

Non-defining (extra information):
Shakespeare, who wrote Hamlet, was born in 1564.

Participle Clauses:

  • Having finished his work, he went home.
  • Written in 1967, the novel became a classic.
  • Being tired, I went to bed early.

Lesson 2.1: Academic and Formal Vocabulary

Advanced Academic Terms:
  • Paradigm: A typical example or pattern
  • Empirical: Based on observation or experience
  • Juxtaposition: Placing things side by side for comparison
  • Ubiquitous: Present everywhere
  • Pragmatic: Practical rather than theoretical
  • Synthesis: Combination of ideas to form a theory
  • Discourse: Written or spoken communication
  • Implicit: Suggested without being directly stated
Practice: Write a paragraph using at least five of these terms in an academic context.

Lesson 2.2: Idiomatic Expressions

Common Idioms in Professional Context:

  • Think outside the box: Be creative and innovative
  • Get the ball rolling: Start a process
  • Hit the ground running: Start with energy and enthusiasm
  • On the same page: In agreement or understanding
  • Cut corners: Do something poorly to save time/money
  • Go the extra mile: Make additional effort
  • Back to the drawing board: Start again from the beginning

Lesson 2.3: Connotation and Register

Understanding Nuance: Words with similar meanings can have different connotations.

Examples:
  • Slender vs. Skinny: Both mean thin, but "slender" is positive while "skinny" can be negative
  • Thrifty vs. Cheap: "Thrifty" is positive (wise with money), "cheap" is negative
  • Assertive vs. Pushy: "Assertive" is positive leadership, "pushy" is aggressive

Register: Adjusting language formality for context.

  • Informal: "I need to think about it."
  • Formal: "I require time to consider this matter."

Lesson 3.1: Academic Writing

Key Features of Academic Writing:

  • Objectivity: Present facts and avoid personal bias
  • Formality: Use appropriate academic register
  • Precision: Be exact and clear in expression
  • Evidence-based: Support claims with citations and data
  • Logical structure: Clear organization and flow

Transition Phrases:

  • Addition: Moreover, Furthermore, Additionally
  • Contrast: However, Nevertheless, Conversely
  • Cause/Effect: Consequently, Therefore, Thus
  • Example: For instance, Specifically, Namely
Practice: Write an academic paragraph analyzing a current social issue, using proper transition phrases and maintaining objectivity.

Lesson 3.2: Rhetorical Strategies

Persuasive Techniques:

  • Ethos: Appeal to credibility and ethics
  • Pathos: Appeal to emotions
  • Logos: Appeal to logic and reason

Example:
"As a physician with 20 years of experience (ethos), I've witnessed the devastating impact of smoking on families (pathos). Studies show that 90% of lung cancer cases are caused by smoking (logos)."

Lesson 3.3: Critical Analysis

Analytical Writing:

Steps for Critical Analysis:
  • 1. Identify the main argument or thesis
  • 2. Examine the evidence presented
  • 3. Evaluate the logic and reasoning
  • 4. Consider alternative perspectives
  • 5. Form and justify your conclusion

Useful Analytical Phrases:

  • The author argues that...
  • This evidence suggests...
  • While this perspective has merit, it fails to consider...
  • A more nuanced interpretation would be...
  • The implications of this argument are...
Practice: Read an opinion article and write a critical analysis evaluating its arguments, evidence, and effectiveness.
